Qualitative Analysis
Themes that surfaced across responses
8 / 12
Harkaway Hills similarity

The most cited concern. Multiple respondents note that the rose, laurel leaves, and overall oval layout closely mirror Harkaway Hills College — making differentiation the Heritage Rose's central problem.

4 / 12
"Rosewood" should be one colour

The two-tone treatment of "ROSE" and "wood" is consistently flagged as confusing — respondents worry it reads as two separate words and undermines the school name.

4 / 12
Border / contrast on the Tree

On dark backgrounds the Living Tree loses definition. A white or light border around the crest is suggested by multiple respondents to improve visibility on uniforms and merchandise.

3 / 12
Banner issue on the Living Tree

The ribbon/banner running through the crest divides opinion. Three respondents independently describe it as unfinished or awkward — with pointed notes about how it might render in embroidery.

3 / 12
Gold elements from the Rose into the Tree

Several Tree-preferrers say they like the gold laurel branches of the Heritage Rose and would want them brought across — suggesting a hybrid enrichment of the winning direction.

3 / 12
Australian identity

Three respondents raise distinctly Australian elements — an Australian native tree variety, or the Southern Cross — as a way to ground the emblem in its local context.

2 / 12
The three-roses proposal

Two respondents suggest three roses as an alternative — keeping the rose motif while solving the Harkaway problem. One articulates a detailed theological rationale connecting three roses to the Trinity, the Theological Virtues, and the wood of the cross.

1 / 12
Classical heraldic structure

One respondent makes a case for moving toward a quartered heraldic shield using classical symbols (open book, torch, dove, vine) drawn from tradition. Both designs were rated 3/5 across all criteria.

1 / 12
The heraldic rose carries deep significance for Opus Dei

One respondent notes that the heraldic rose is not merely a reference to the school's name — it holds profound institutional meaning within the Opus Dei prelature, where the rose features in the coat of arms and carries theological and spiritual symbolism tied to the charism of the Work. This adds a layer of significance to the Heritage Rose direction that goes beyond aesthetics or name-matching, and is worth the board weighing explicitly in their deliberations.

Q9 — Non-negotiables for the designer

What the board wants the final mark to achieve — regardless of direction

  • A parent should understand the emblem without it being explained to them — symbol and meaning must be immediate.
  • It must be visually distinct from other PARED schools, particularly Harkaway Hills College.
  • The name "Rosewood" should read as a single word — one colour, consistent treatment.
  • The design must hold up across all applications: embroidery, uniforms, letterhead, signage, and small-scale print.
  • It should feel like Rosewood specifically — personal, family-partnered, and virtue-forming — not a generic school crest.
Note on the Heritage Rose responses

Of the three respondents who selected the Heritage Rose, two noted significant reservations about the current design — including the Harkaway similarity and the need for more visual richness. Only one respondent gave the Heritage Rose strong ratings across all criteria. The Heritage Rose preference appears to reflect attachment to the rose motif rather than confidence in the current execution.

One respondent raises an additional dimension: the heraldic rose carries deep and specific meaning within the Opus Dei prelature, appearing in its coat of arms and connected to the charism of the Work. This institutional significance is distinct from the school-name connection and represents a consideration the board may wish to weigh independently of the visual and competitive arguments.
