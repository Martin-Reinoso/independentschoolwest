Proposed by one respondent
In Sapientia et Virtute
Translation: "In wisdom and virtue"
Why it reflects Rosewood

Rosewood is a place where students are formed within an environment of wisdom and virtue. It speaks of the school as a community grounded in the Catholic understanding that education is not merely the transfer of knowledge, but the formation of the whole person: intellect, character, and spiritual life, resonating deeply with our school philosophy (i.e., learning forms "intellect, character, and spiritual life," enabling students to grow "in wisdom, virtue, and freedom," and that academic excellence is pursued within a Catholic worldview integrating "faith and reason, truth, goodness, and beauty.")

In short, wisdom directs the mind toward truth; virtue forms the heart and will toward goodness. Together, they reflect the College's mission to educate the whole person within a Catholic vision of life, where faith and reason, character and learning, freedom and responsibility are united.

Rosewood is not simply aiming to produce clever students, nor merely well-behaved students, but young people who are inwardly formed: able to recognise truth, choose the good freely, love God and others, and use their gifts in service.

What should be reflected
Wisdom directs the mind toward truth; virtue forms the heart and will toward goodness. Together they express Rosewood's commitment to forming the whole person within a Catholic vision of life.
Proposed by one respondent
Dignitas, Caritas, Veritas
Translation: "Dignity, Love, Truth"
Why it reflects Rosewood

This motto offers a deeply Catholic vision of education and human formation. Rooted in the Gospel and the intellectual tradition of the Church, it expresses the essential values that shape both the individual person and the wider school community.

  • Dignitas reminds students who they are.
  • Caritas teaches them how they are called to live.
  • Veritas guides them toward what they are called to seek.

It is both timeless and aspirational — intellectually strong, spiritually rich, and clearly Catholic without being inaccessible. The Latin phrasing gives the motto a sense of tradition, permanence, and academic distinction consistent with the heritage of Catholic education.

What should be reflected
A community grounded in human dignity, animated by Christian love, and dedicated to truth. The formation of the whole person.

Fons Vitae Caritas
Translation: "Love is the fountain of life"
Why it reflects Rosewood — Respondent A

Fons Vitae Caritas translates directly to "Love is the fountain of life" or "Charity is the source of life." It is a reminder that love is the highest virtue that leads us to our heavenly destination.

It can be understood by non-Catholics and Catholics alike — because humanly and earthly speaking, love is the purpose and source of life here on earth too.

What should be reflected
The importance of human virtues, which are the basis of the supernatural virtues. Our purpose in life.
Why it reflects Rosewood — Respondent B

Fons Vitae Caritas is a fitting motto for a Catholic school shaped by the vision of the Second Vatican Council, and the teachings of Saint John Paul the Great and Saint Josemaría.

This motto expresses the school's conviction that education is not simply about academic success, but about forming people who live virtuously, serve others, and contribute to society. It reflects Christ's teaching that the world would know his disciples by their love for one another (Jn 13:35).

The phrase also resonates strongly with St Josemaría's emphasis on sanctifying ordinary life through work, duty, discipline, and service. Love is not presented here as sentimentality, but as the animating principle of a meaningful life — the force that inspires excellence, civic responsibility, generosity, and self-mastery.

The motto is also especially appropriate for the school's future identity as a girls' school. John Paul II taught that women possess a particular strength in the "order of love" — a unique attentiveness to the dignity and flourishing of persons. Fons Vitae Caritas captures this insight in a way that is aspirational, dignified, and deeply Catholic.

Finally, the image of a fountain is rich in symbolism. A fountain gives life to everything around it. In the same way, the school aims to form young people whose faith, virtue, leadership, and charity become a source of life for their families, professions, communities, and nation.

What should be reflected
The world needs the reminder that intelligence, confidence, virtue, and civic duty flow from love. If our students live by a motto that captures that, then we are doing what we can to turn the tide in the right direction.
Proposed by one respondent · companion to In Sapientia et Virtute
Virtute et Sapientia Crescimus
Translation: "Through virtue and wisdom we grow"
Why it reflects Rosewood

This motto is a more dynamic and personal take on In Sapientia et Virtute. It emphasises growth, which is central to Rosewood's philosophy: each child is known, valued, mentored, and capable of growth.

The Latin crescimus ("we grow") gives the motto a communal tone: students, staff, and families grow together, which fits beautifully with Rosewood's mission to partner with parents in a personalised Catholic education grounded in "faith, virtue, and academic excellence," nurturing the intellectual, moral, and spiritual development of each student.

What should be reflected
  • Growth — because children are on a journey of formation
  • Wisdom — because education seeks truth, not just information
  • Virtue — because character gives direction to freedom
  • Freedom — because virtue is not control, but the capacity to choose the good
  • Service — because wisdom and virtue should bear fruit in love for others
